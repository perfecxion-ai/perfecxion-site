But what about when your AI agent, with full authorization, accesses that database **10,000 times in an hour** because it misunderstood its objective?

### The Baseline Problem
Security teams love baselines. Normal behavior patterns that help identify anomalies. But agentic AI systems don't have baselinesthey have objectives. Their behavior changes based on:
- Current goals and sub-goals
- Environmental conditions
- Learning from previous actions
- Interactions with other agents
- Resource availability
- Success/failure feedback loops

### Risk 3: Emergent Behavior Cascades
The most dangerous risks arise from emergent behaviorspatterns that weren't programmed but develop through the interaction of simple rules at scale. These behaviors are impossible to predict and often invisible until they reach critical mass.



<div class="bg-purple-50 dark:bg-purple-900/20 border-l-4 border-purple-500 p-6 mb-8 rounded-r-lg">

<p class="text-purple-800 dark:text-purple-300 leading-relaxed">
Individual agents following simple rules create complex system behaviors. Like murmurations of starlings, the collective behavior becomes something entirely different from individual actions. Your monitoring sees thousands of normal transactions, while missing the emergent pattern that's <strong>restructuring your entire business logic</strong>.

### Dynamic Trust Boundaries
Instead of static permissions, AI agents need dynamic trust boundaries that adjust based on:
- Current behavior patterns
- Historical performance
- Environmental risk factors
- Potential impact scope
- Confidence levels in decisions
